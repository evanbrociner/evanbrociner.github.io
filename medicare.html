Medicare 101

Source: Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Nebraska
Term Description
Annual Election Period (AEP) Begins October 15 and ends December 7 each year. During AEP, you can enroll in, disenroll from, or change your Medicare Advantage plan or prescription drug plan. You can also return to Original Medicare. Elections made during AEP are effective January 1 of the following year.
Annual Notice of Changes (ANOC) A notice sent each September by Medicare Advantage plans to their members. It details any changes in coverage, costs, or service for the upcoming plan year.
Coinsurance A percentage of medical and drug costs you may pay as your share for services or supplies (e.g., 20% of a prescription drug cost).
Copayment A fixed dollar amount you may pay as your share for services or supplies (e.g., a $10 copay for a doctor visit).
Coverage gap ("donut hole") For 2024 plans, begins after you and your drug plan have spent $5,030 on covered drugs. While in the gap, you pay a larger share of drug costs. Members will pay no more than 25% for brand-name or generic drugs until they reach the out-of-pocket spending limit ($8,000 in 2024). From 2024 on, members pay $0 for catastrophic coverage.
Creditable prescription drug coverage Coverage from another source (e.g., employer benefits) that is at least as good as Medicare Part D.
Deductible Amount you pay for medical services or drugs in a plan year before your plan starts to pay for benefits.
Drug List (Formulary) A list of specific drugs covered by a prescription drug plan, often divided into tiers based on cost-sharing amounts.
Evidence of Coverage (EOC) A document detailing your Medicare plan's benefits and costs.
Health Maintenance Organization (HMO) A type of health insurance plan where a primary care physician coordinates your care using providers in the plan's network.
Initial Coverage Election Period (ICEP) A 7-month period when a Medicare-eligible person can sign up for Medicare for the first time. It begins 3 months before your 65th birthday, includes your birthday month, and ends 3 months after.
Medicaid Health coverage for certain low-income people, including pregnant women, the elderly, and those with disabilities. Jointly funded by federal and state dollars.
Medicare Advantage (Medicare Part C) Plans offered by private insurers approved by Medicare. Must offer, at minimum, the same benefits as Original Medicare (Parts A and B). Most also include prescription drug coverage, and many include some coverage for dental, vision and hearing.
Medicare Part A Part of Original Medicare, helps cover inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice care, and some home healthcare.
Medicare Part B Part of Original Medicare, helps cover doctors' services, preventive services, outpatient care, and medical supplies.
Medicare Part D Prescription drug insurance sold through private insurance companies. Can be offered as part of a Medicare Advantage plan or as a stand-alone prescription drug plan (PDP).
Medicare Supplement Insurance (Medigap) Sold by private insurance companies to help cover out-of-pocket costs not paid by Original Medicare, like copays and deductibles. Does not include Part D prescription drug coverage.
Network A group of healthcare providers who agree to provide care based on a plan's terms. Includes doctors, hospitals, and other healthcare professionals and facilities. You usually save money by using in-network providers.
Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (OEP) For people enrolled in Medicare Advantage only. Runs from January 1 to March 31 each year. Members can switch to another Medicare Advantage plan or return to Original Medicare.
Original Medicare (Traditional Medicare) Offered directly by the federal government. Consists of Medicare Part A (hospital) and Part B (medical) coverage.
Out-of-pocket costs Costs you pay for medical care, prescriptions, etc. Includes coinsurance, copayments, and deductibles. Medicare Advantage plans have annual maximum out-of-pocket limits. |
Preferred Provider Organization (PPO) A type of health insurance plan that lets you choose your own doctors and hospitals. Out-of-pocket costs are generally lower if you use in-network providers.
Premium The monthly amount you pay to Medicare or your private insurer for healthcare coverage.
Special Needs Plan (SNP) A Medicare Advantage plan that includes Parts A, B and D benefits and may have additional benefits. To qualify, you must have Medicare Parts A and B, and either have a verified chronic illness (C-SNP), receive Medicaid (D-SNP), or live in a long-term care facility (I-SNP).

Senior Care

Resource Description
Venturing in Senior Care: Observations and Predictions for 2024 and Beyond

Daniel Kaplan writes how senior living and healthcare are merging, affordability remains a major challenge, and new care models are emerging to address these issues.

Key Points
  • Merging of Senior Living and Healthcare: Senior living facilities are increasingly providing healthcare services due to the rising acuity of residents. This trend is expected to continue as the population ages.
  • Affordability Crisis: The article highlights the challenge of many seniors not being able to afford assisted living, nursing homes, or even basic care. This is a major issue that needs solutions.
  • New Care Models: The emergence of new models like disease-specific care and wrap-around care models offer promise for improving the quality of care for seniors while potentially reducing costs.
Dying Broke: A KFF Health News-New York Times Project

KFF Health News and The New York Times examine how the immense financial costs of long-term care drain older Americans and their families.

America's Seniors and the Challenges of Old Age

In this article the NYTimes writes that the US is aging rapidly and needs to prepare for the social and economic consequences, including potential labor shortages, strain on social programs, and the need for more elder care.

More American Seniors Live Alone

People over 60 are more likely to live alone in the United States and Europe than in other regions of the world.

Why Are Older Americans Drinking So Much?

The pandemic played a role in increased consumption, but alcohol use among people 65 and older was climbing even before 2020.

Long-Term Care: Last Week Tonight with John Oliver

John Oliver explains the industry behind nursing homes and assisted living facilities, and why long-term care needs fixing.

How Do We Fix the Scandal That Is American Health Care?

Nicholas Kristof argues that the U.S. has a healthcare crisis despite advanced technology, and improving health requires addressing access, behavior, and social determinants.

Key Points
  • Poor health outcomes in the US compared to other wealthy nations, despite advanced medical technology. (e.g. avoidable diabetic amputations)
  • Contributing factors: lack of healthcare access, unhealthy behaviors, social determinants of health (poverty, education).
  • Solutions: expand healthcare access, promote healthy behaviors, address social determinants of health (e.g. Delta Health Alliance program).
